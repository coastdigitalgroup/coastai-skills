# Discounting Persuasion Principles

## 1. The Rule of 100
- **Under $100:** Use percentage discounts (e.g., "20% Off"). The number usually feels larger to the consumer.
- **Over $100:** Use absolute dollar discounts (e.g., "$25 Off"). At this scale, the dollar amount often feels more significant than the percentage.

## 2. The Endowment Effect
- Frame the discount as something the user already "owns" or has "earned."
- Use: "Claim your $10 credit" instead of "Use code SAVE10."

## 3. The Zeigarnik Effect (Tiered Rewards)
- Humans have a psychological urge to complete unfinished tasks.
- Using a progress bar for tiered discounts (e.g., "You're $12 away from $20 off") creates a "mental itch" that the user wants to scratch by adding more items.

## 4. Specificity & Scarcity
- Unique, one-time codes (e.g., `WELCOME-XY72`) have a higher perceived value than generic codes (e.g., `WELCOME10`).
- Users are less likely to share unique codes, maintaining your margin control.

## 5. Framing the Savings
- Always show the "Original Price" struck through next to the "New Price."
- Explicitly state the "Total Saved" at the bottom of the cart to reinforce the reward.
