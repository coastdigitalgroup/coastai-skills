# Psychological Principles for Interactive Demos

To maximize the impact of an interactive product tour, apply these core UX
heuristics and psychological triggers.

## 1. The Endowment Effect
When users "interact" with a product—by clicking, typing, or moving elements—
they begin to feel a sense of ownership over the experience.
- **Application:** Instead of "Watch this video," use "Type your name to see
  how the dashboard looks for you."

## 2. The Zeigarnik Effect
People remember uncompleted or interrupted tasks better than completed ones.
- **Application:** Use a progress bar. Seeing "Step 2 of 5" creates a
  psychological "open loop" that the user feels compelled to close by finishing
  the tour.

## 3. The Labor Illusion
People value a result more when they feel like they've seen the "work" being
done.
- **Application:** Use subtle loading states between complex "value" steps
  (e.g., "Analyzing your data...") to make the final result feel more
  personalized and "earned."

## 4. Hick's Law
The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity
of choices.
- **Application:** Minimize the UI shown in the demo. Blur out or remove
  distracting sidebars and menus that aren't relevant to the current step of
  the narrative.

## 5. Peak-End Rule
People judge an experience largely based on how they felt at its peak and at
its end.
- **Application:** Ensure your highest-value feature (the "Peak") is in the
  middle of the tour, and your final CTA (the "End") is celebratory and
  frictionless.

## 6. Information Scent
Users will continue a journey as long as they feel the "scent" of the reward is
getting stronger.
- **Application:** Every step must clearly state what the *next* step will
  reveal. (e.g., "Next: See how much budget you can save").
