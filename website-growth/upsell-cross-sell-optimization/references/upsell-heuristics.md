# Upsell & Cross-sell Heuristics

Effective upselling and cross-selling is a balance of psychology, timing, and
user experience. Use these heuristics to design offers that feel helpful, not
harassing.

## 1. The "Primacy of Choice" Heuristic

Users are most receptive to additional offers *after* they have made their
primary decision but *before* they have completed the final transaction.

- **Peak Intent:** The moment a user clicks "Add to Cart" is a peak of intent.
  Small, highly relevant cross-sells here have the highest take rate.
- **The Decision Pivot:** If you offer an upsell (a more expensive version),
  do it on the product page *before* they commit to the cheaper version.
  Offering a more expensive version *after* they've added to cart often causes
  them to second-guess their original choice and abandon the cart.

## 2. The Decoy Effect (Asymmetric Dominance)

When choosing between two options (A and B), users often struggle. Adding a
third, "decoy" option (C) that is clearly inferior to B but priced similarly
makes B look like a massive bargain.

- **Example:**
  - Option A: Digital Only ($50)
  - Option B: Print + Digital ($125) -> *The Target*
  - Option C: Print Only ($125) -> *The Decoy*
- **Outcome:** Most people will choose Option B because it feels like they are
  getting the Digital version for "free."

## 3. Threshold Proximity (Goal Gradient Effect)

People are more motivated to complete a task as they get closer to the goal.

- **The Shipping Hook:** "You're only $12 away from Free Shipping."
- **The Discount Hook:** "Spend $10 more to unlock 20% off your entire order."
- **UX Pattern:** Use a visual progress bar in the cart or a sticky banner to
  keep the goal visible.

## 4. The Endowment Effect (Bundling)

Users value things more once they feel they "own" them. By presenting a bundle
where the items are already "packaged" together, the user feels like they are
*losing* something if they remove an item, rather than *gaining* something by
adding it.

- **Heuristic:** Default to the bundle if it provides a clear, discounted
  "Value Buy," but make it effortless to revert to single items.

## 5. Reciprocity & "Thank You" Offers

The post-purchase "Thank You" page is the most under-utilized real estate in
e-commerce. The user has just completed a "favor" (buying from you) and is
feeling a "post-purchase high."

- **The One-Click Rule:** At this stage, cognitive load must be zero. The offer
  must be "One-Click" and require no new data entry.
- **The "Logic Gap":** The post-purchase offer should be a "Next Logical Step"
  (e.g., if they bought a printer, offer paper; if they bought a course, offer
  a coaching call).

## 6. The "Three-to-One" Visual Weight

The primary CTA (completing the purchase) should always have significantly more
visual weight (size, contrast, color) than the upsell/cross-sell offer.

- **Heuristic:** If the user has to squint to find the "Checkout" button because
  of a large "Special Offer" banner, the hierarchy is broken.
- **Micro-Copy:** Use "Add" or "Upgrade" for the offer, but keep "Checkout" or
  "Complete Order" as the anchor action.
