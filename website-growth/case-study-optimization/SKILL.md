# Case Study Optimization

## Purpose

The Case Study Optimization skill provides a protocol for auditing and
restructuring customer success stories to maximize their impact on the sales
funnel. It moves away from "wall-of-text" narratives toward result-first,
scannable layouts that prove value through the PSR (Problem-Solution-Result)
framework. This skill directly impacts the conversion rate of high-intent
visitors by providing the specific proof they need to justify a purchase or
demo request.

## Use Cases

- **B2B SaaS:** Demonstrating technical ROI and implementation success for
  complex products.
- **Service Agencies:** Showcasing client wins to validate expertise and
  delivery capability.
- **Enterprise Sales:** Providing "social proof" tailored to specific industries
  or technical roles.
- **Middle-of-Funnel (MoFu) Content:** Supporting users who are comparing
  solutions and need evidence of success.

## When NOT to Use

- **Early-Stage Testimonials:** Single-sentence quotes or broad praise should be
  handled via `social-proof-optimization`.
- **General Blog Posts:** Educational content or thought leadership doesn't
  follow the strict proof-based structure of a case study.
- **Transactional E-commerce:** While product reviews are important, they don't
  require the full narrative depth of a business case study.

## Inputs

1. **Raw Customer Data:** Interviews, performance metrics, "before and after"
   snapshots, and direct quotes.
2. **Target Persona:** Who is the case study meant to convince? (e.g., CFOs care
   about ROI; Engineers care about implementation speed).
3. **The "Hero" Plan:** Which product or service tier was used to achieve the
   result?
4. **Current Performance:** Time-on-page and CTR from the existing case study to
   the primary CTA.

## Outputs

1. **Case Study Audit:** Identification of narrative "fluff" and missed proof
   opportunities.
2. **Result-First Structure:** A revised layout starting with the most
   compelling metric or outcome.
3. **PSR Framework Content:** Refined copy following the Problem, Solution, and
   measurable Result sequence.
4. **Conversion-Optimized Layout:** Guidance on placement of "pull quotes," data
   visualizations, and context-aware CTAs.

## Workflow

### 1. Identify the "North Star" Metric

Every case study needs one headline result.
- **Quantitative:** "Increased revenue by 40%," "Reduced churn by 15%."
- **Qualitative:** "Saved 20 hours per week for the HR team."
- **Rule:** If you don't have a specific result, the case study is just a story.

### 2. Apply the PSR (Problem-Solution-Result) Framework

Structure the narrative to mirror the user's journey:
- **Problem:** Define the pain points in the customer's own words. Why was the
  status quo unsustainable?
- **Solution:** Describe how the product/service was implemented. Focus on the
  *specific features* that solved the problem.
- **Result:** Present the data. Use "pull quotes" and bold text to make the wins
  unmissable.

### 3. Design for the "Skimmer"

Most users won't read the whole study.
- **Summary Box:** Include a "tl;dr" sidebar with the key stats and the "Hero"
  product used.
- **Pull Quotes:** Use large, bold quotes from the customer that address
  specific objections (e.g., "The setup took only 2 hours").
- **Data Visualization:** Turn complex wins into simple charts or "Before vs.
  After" comparisons.

### 4. Implement Contextual CTAs

Don't just put one button at the bottom.
- **The "Inline" CTA:** After a major result is shared, add a CTA: "Get the same
  results for your team."
- **The "Sticky" Sidebar:** Keep the primary CTA (e.g., "Book a Demo") visible as
  the user scrolls through the success story.

### 5. Review Against Decision Rules

Ensure the case study prioritizes evidence over marketing jargon.

## Decision Rules

- **Result-First Rule:** The headline and first paragraph must state the outcome.
  Don't make the user "dig" for the win.
- **Specifics Over Superlatives:** Use "42% increase" instead of "a huge
  increase." Use "Fortune 500 Retailer" instead of "A large company."
- **The Relatability Rule:** Group case studies by industry or use case so
  visitors can find a "mirror" of their own situation.
- **No Fluff:** If a sentence doesn't explain the problem, the solution, or the
  result, delete it.

## Constraints

- **Customer Approval:** All quotes and metrics must be approved by the featured
  customer to maintain trust and legal compliance.
- **Data Integrity:** Never "round up" or fabricate results; one debunked case
  study destroys the credibility of the entire site.
- **Recency:** Case studies older than 2 years should be audited for relevance
  or archived if the product has significantly changed.

## Non-Goals

- Writing the initial draft or conducting the customer interview.
- Video production for case studies (see `video-conversion-optimization`).
- SEO keyword stuffing at the expense of narrative clarity.

## Common Failure Patterns

- **The "Company Biography":** Spending half the page talking about the
  customer's history instead of the problem they solved.
- **Vague Outcomes:** Saying "Efficiency improved" without saying by how much or
  how it was measured.
- **Technical Overload:** Explaining the *how* so deeply that the business
  value (the *why*) is lost.
- **Dead Ends:** Not providing a clear next step for the reader after they see
  the success.

## Validation Criteria

- [ ] **CTR to CTA:** Measure the percentage of readers who click the CTA within
  the case study. Target: >5% for B2B.
- [ ] **Time-on-Page:** Compare "optimized" case studies against older versions
  to see if engagement increases.
- [ ] **Lead Attribution:** Track if qualified leads mentioned the case study in
  their initial discovery call or form "How did you hear about us?"
- [ ] **The "Skim Test":** Can a user understand the "Problem" and "Result" in
  under 15 seconds of scrolling?
