# Reference: Common Web Scanning Patterns

Understanding how users "read" screens is essential for placing high-value
information where it will actually be seen.

## 1. The F-Pattern
**Used for:** Content-heavy pages, blog posts, and search results.

- **The Path:** Users start at the top-left, read across the top, then drop
  down and read across a shorter distance, then scan vertically down the
  left-hand side.
- **Optimization Strategy:**
  - Place your "Hook" in the first two paragraphs.
  - Use benefit-driven words at the *start* of headlines and bullet points.
  - Keep important navigation or CTAs on the left side or top bar.

## 2. The Z-Pattern
**Used for:** Simple landing pages, login screens, and "Coming Soon" pages.

- **The Path:** The eye follows a "Z" shape: Top-left (Logo) -> Top-right
  (Action) -> Diagonal to Bottom-left (Hero Text) -> Bottom-right (Final CTA).
- **Optimization Strategy:**
  - Put the "Big Idea" in the middle of the Z.
  - End the Z-path with your primary Call to Action.
  - Don't clutter the diagonal path with distracting secondary elements.

## 3. The Layer-Cake Pattern
**Used for:** Feature pages, "How it Works" sections, and long-form sales pages.

- **The Path:** The user "skims" the page by looking only at headlines and
  subheadings. They skip the body text entirely unless a headline catches
  their attention.
- **Optimization Strategy:**
  - Make sure your H2s tell a complete story on their own.
  - Use large, bold, and high-contrast typography for subheadings.
  - If a user *only* read the H2s, would they still understand the value?

## 4. The Spotted Pattern
**Used for:** Pricing tables, technical spec sheets, and comparison guides.

- **The Path:** The user's eye "jumps" around the page looking for specific
  visual cues like numbers, price tags, bold words, or colored icons.
- **Optimization Strategy:**
  - Use **Bolding** for the most important data points.
  - Use icons to signify categories (e.g., a "Shield" icon for security).
  - Highlight the "Most Popular" option in a pricing grid to anchor the
    spotted scan.

## 5. The Commitment Gap (The "Fold")
Regardless of the pattern, attention drops off significantly after the
initial "screen-full" (the fold).

- **Rule:** Place the most critical information and at least one primary
  pathway (CTA) within the first 600-800 pixels of vertical height.
- **Exception:** For high-complexity products, use a "Curiosity Gap"
  (directional cue) to encourage the user to scroll into a Layer-cake scan.
