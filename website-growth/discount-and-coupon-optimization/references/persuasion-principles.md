# Behavioral Economics in Discounting

Understanding the psychology behind how users perceive value is critical for
optimizing discounts.

## 1. The Rule of 100
This principle states that the perceived value of a discount depends on the
numerical "weight" of the framing:
- **Low-Price Items (< $100):** A percentage discount feels larger because the
  number is usually higher. (e.g., "25% Off" a $20 item sounds better than "$5
  Off").
- **High-Price Items (> $100):** A dollar-off discount feels larger. (e.g., "$100
  Off" a $1,000 item sounds better than "10% Off").

## 2. The Zeigarnik Effect
The human brain has a powerful desire to complete unfinished tasks. In
discounting, this is leveraged via **Progress Bars**:
- Showing a user they are "80% of the way to Free Shipping" creates a "mental
  itch" that they want to resolve by adding one more item to their cart.

## 3. The Endowment Effect
People value things more once they feel they "own" them.
- Framing a discount as **"Your $10 Credit"** or **"Claim Your Savings"** makes
  the user feel like the discount is already theirs, and not using it feels
  like a loss.

## 4. Loss Aversion
The pain of losing is twice as powerful as the joy of gaining.
- Using urgency triggers like **"Your discount expires in 14:59"** triggers the
  fear of losing a financial advantage, which is a stronger motivator than the
  savings themselves.

## 5. Decision Paralysis (Hick's Law)
Too many choices can lead to no choice at all.
- Offering 5 different coupon codes for different categories often leads to
  the user using none of them. Stick to **one clear primary offer** per session.
