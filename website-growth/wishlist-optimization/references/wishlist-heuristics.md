# Wishlist Heuristics & Persuasion Principles

These heuristics provide the psychological and UX foundation for high-performing
wishlist and "save-for-later" experiences.

## 1. Capturing "Soft Intent"
Not every user is ready to buy on their first visit. The wishlist acts as a
"micro-conversion"—a lower-stakes action that signals interest without
committing to a purchase.
- **Goal:** Minimize the "cost" of the micro-conversion.
- **Application:** Use Guest Wishlists to remove the friction of account
  creation.

## 2. Reducing Choice Paralysis (Hick’s Law)
In the discovery phase, users often feel overwhelmed by choices. Saving items
allows them to "narrow the field" and focus on a subset of products they
actually like.
- **Goal:** Facilitate comparison and elimination.
- **Application:** Allow users to view saved items side-by-side or in a
  concentrated list.

## 3. The Zeigarnik Effect (Unfinished Tasks)
Humans have a better memory for incomplete or interrupted tasks than for
completed ones. A saved item represents an "unfinished purchase" in the
user's mind.
- **Goal:** Create a "mental itch" to return.
- **Application:** Show a small badge count (e.g., "3 items") on the wishlist
  icon in the header to remind the user of their saved progress.

## 4. Endowment Effect (Sense of Ownership)
The more a user interacts with a product—saving it, organizing it into a list,
monitoring its price—the more they begin to feel a sense of ownership over it.
- **Goal:** Increase the perceived value of the saved item.
- **Application:** Use personal language like "Your Wishlist" or "My
  Collection" to reinforce this psychological connection.

## 5. Loss Aversion & Urgency
Once an item is saved, users are more sensitive to losing the opportunity to
buy it.
- **Goal:** Drive the final conversion through timely alerts.
- **Application:** Send notifications for "Low Stock" or "Price Increasing
  Soon" specifically for items in the user's wishlist.

## 6. Recognition Over Recall
Users should not have to remember which items they liked in a previous session.
- **Goal:** Minimize cognitive load.
- **Application:** Display "Recently Saved" items on the homepage for
  returning visitors to provide immediate continuity.

## 7. The Subordination Principle (UX Hierarchy)
The wishlist is a secondary action. It should support the primary "Buy" goal,
not replace it.
- **Goal:** Prevent the wishlist from becoming a distraction for high-intent
  users.
- **Application:** The "Add to Wishlist" button should be visually distinct
  from and less prominent than the "Add to Cart" button (e.g., using a
  ghost button or an icon-only approach).
