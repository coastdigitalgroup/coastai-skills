# Retention Principles for Cancel Flows

Applying these psychological and UX principles to a cancellation flow helps shift the focus from "losing a user" to "solving a user's problem."

## 1. Loss Aversion
Users are more motivated to avoid losing something they already have than to gain something of equal value.
- **Application:** Remind the user of the data they have created, the reports they have saved, or the "Grandfathered" pricing they will lose.
- **Example:** "You've saved 42 hours this month using ProjectStream. If you cancel, your automation workflows will be deleted."

## 2. The Sunk Cost Fallacy (Ethical Application)
Users are more likely to continue an endeavor if they have already invested time, money, or effort.
- **Application:** Highlight the "setup" work they have already done.
- **Example:** "It took 15 minutes to sync your accounts. If you cancel now, you'll have to start over if you return."

## 3. The Reciprocity Principle
When you give someone something of value, they feel an obligation to give something back.
- **Application:** Offer a "free month" or a deep discount as a goodwill gesture before they leave.
- **Example:** "We'd hate for price to be the reason you leave. Here is 1 month on us to help you get through your next project."

## 4. Choice Architecture (The "Golden Bridge")
By offering an attractive alternative (the "Golden Bridge"), you make it easier for the user to retreat from the "extreme" action of cancellation.
- **Application:** Present "Pause" or "Downgrade" as the middle path between "Pay Full Price" and "Delete Everything."

## 5. The Zeigarnik Effect
People remember uncompleted or interrupted tasks better than completed ones.
- **Application:** If a user is mid-way through a project or a trial, remind them of the "unfinished" value.
- **Example:** "You still have 3 active projects in progress. Why not finish them before you go?"

## 6. Reactive vs. Proactive Retention
- **Reactive Retention:** Trying to save a user at the moment they click "Cancel."
- **Proactive Retention:** Identifying users with "low usage" or "low health scores" and intervening *before* they ever reach the cancel page.
- **Skill Focus:** This skill focuses on **Reactive Retention**, providing the last line of defense against churn.

## 7. The "Hotel California" UX Pattern (Avoid This)
A "Dark Pattern" where it is incredibly easy to sign up but nearly impossible to cancel (requiring phone calls, multiple transfers, etc.).
- **Impact:** While it may lower short-term churn, it destroys brand reputation, leads to negative reviews, and increases credit card chargebacks.
- **Recommendation:** Always make the exit "persuasive" but "fair."
