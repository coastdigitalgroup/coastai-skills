# The Psychology of Interactive Demos

To maximize the impact of a self-guided product tour, you must understand the psychological principles that drive user engagement and conversion.

## 1. The Endowment Effect
The Endowment Effect is the tendency for people to value something more highly simply because they own it.
- **Application:** By allowing users to interact with your UI, click buttons, and "trigger" results, you create a sense of psychological ownership.
- **Optimization:** Use possessive language in tooltips (e.g., "Customize *your* view" or "Invite *your* team") to reinforce this feeling.

## 2. The Zeigarnik Effect
The Zeigarnik Effect states that people remember uncompleted or interrupted tasks better than completed ones, and they feel a psychological drive to finish what they've started.
- **Application:** This is why progress bars are so effective in interactive demos.
- **Optimization:** Always show "Step X of Y." If a user leaves at Step 7 of 10, they are more likely to return later than if they had no idea how close they were to the finish.

## 3. The Labor Illusion
The Labor Illusion is the phenomenon where people value a service more if they can see the effort or "work" going into it.
- **Application:** In a demo, an instantaneous result can sometimes feel "too easy" or like a "fake" simulation.
- **Optimization:** Adding a brief (1-2 second) loading state with copy like "Analyzing your data..." or "Syncing with your CRM..." increases the perceived value of the final result.

## 4. The Peak-End Rule
Psychologist Daniel Kahneman found that people judge an experience largely based on how they felt at its peak (the most intense point) and at its end, rather than the average of every moment.
- **Application:** Most demos are a flat line of "Feature 1, Feature 2, Feature 3."
- **Optimization:** Ensure your demo has a "climax"—a screen that shows the most impressive result—and a strong "End" that makes the user feel successful and clear about the next steps.

## 5. Cognitive Load & The Power of Simplicity
Miller's Law suggests the average person can keep 7 (plus or minus 2) items in their working memory.
- **Application:** A product tour that shows 30 features will be forgotten entirely.
- **Optimization:** Limit your demo to a single "Job to be Done" with no more than 12 steps. Focus on the *outcome*, not the *interface*.
