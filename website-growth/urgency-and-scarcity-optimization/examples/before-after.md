# Example: Urgency and Scarcity Applied to E-commerce

This example demonstrates how an e-commerce store selling premium outdoor gear
optimized their product page to reduce procrastination using authentic urgency
and scarcity.

## The Scenario

**Product:** High-performance camping tent ($450).
**Problem:** High traffic from ads, but users were "window shopping." The "Add
to Cart" rate was healthy (8%), but the "Checkout Completion" rate was low
(15% of carts converted). Many users returned 3-4 times before buying, or never
returned at all.

## Before Optimization

- **PDP:** Clear images, detailed specs, "Add to Cart" button.
- **Messaging:** Standard "In Stock" indicator.
- **Cart:** Standard item list.
- **Outcome:** Users felt they could "think about it" and return later, leading
  to a high drop-off as they checked competitors or simply forgot.

## The Optimization (The "Active Decision" Strategy)

1. **Shipping Deadline Urgency:** Added a dynamic timer near the "Add to Cart"
   button: *"Order in the next 3h 42m for Guaranteed Delivery by Friday."*
2. **Low Stock Scarcity:** Instead of "In Stock," the system displayed real
   numbers when stock dropped below 10: *"Only 4 left in stock - 12 people are
   viewing this now."*
3. **Cart Reservation Reassurance:** In the cart, added a subtle note: *"High
   demand: We cannot reserve items. Complete your order now to secure your
   tent."*
4. **Limited-Time Bundle:** Offered a 10% discount on a footprint (ground
   cloth) if added within the same session.

## After Optimization

- **Measurable Outcome:**
  - **Add-to-Cart Rate:** Increased from 8% to 10.5% (Relative lift: +31%).
  - **Checkout Completion:** Increased from 15% to 22% (Relative lift: +46%).
  - **Average Time-to-Purchase:** Dropped from 4.2 days to 1.8 days.
  - **Overall Conversion:** +25% lift in total revenue.

## Why it Worked

The optimization replaced a "passive browsing" state with an "active decision"
state. By providing a clear benefit for acting now (delivery date) and a clear
risk of waiting (losing stock), the user was forced to decide if they wanted
the product today rather than deferring the decision indefinitely.
