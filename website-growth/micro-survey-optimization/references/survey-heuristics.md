# Micro-Survey Heuristics & Persuasion Principles

Effective micro-surveys leverage psychology to maximize participation and accuracy. Use these principles to guide your survey design.

## 1. The Zeigarnik Effect (Open Loops)
Users are more likely to finish a task they have already started.
- **Application:** Use a simple, one-click first question. Once they've clicked, they are significantly more likely to fill out a second, open-ended question.

## 2. Reducing Cognitive Load
Don't make the user "think" more than necessary.
- **Application:** Use clear, simple language. Avoid industry jargon. If a question is too complex, break it into two simpler parts.

## 3. Reciprocity
If you give something, users feel an obligation to give back.
- **Application:** On informational pages, provide a "Value-Add" (like a PDF download) before or after the survey to increase brand affinity and future response rates.

## 4. Specificity over Generality
Specific questions get specific (and actionable) answers.
- **Vague:** "How was your experience?" -> **Specific:** "Was there anything confusing about our pricing tiers?"

## 5. The "Foot-in-the-Door" Technique
A small request paves the way for a larger one.
- **Application:** Start with a simple "Yes/No" or "Rating" before asking for an email or detailed feedback.

## 6. Saliency
The survey must feel relevant to the current task.
- **Application:** A survey about "Checkout Friction" is highly salient on the checkout page but irrelevant (and annoying) on the "About Us" page.

## 7. Peak-End Rule
People judge an experience largely based on how they felt at its peak and at its end.
- **Application:** Ensure the "Thank You" screen is warm, helpful, and provides a clear next step (e.g., "Back to shopping," "Download your guide").
