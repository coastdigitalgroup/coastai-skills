# SCTA Persuasion Principles

The effectiveness of Secondary Calls-to-Action (SCTAs) is rooted in several
well-established psychological and UX principles. Understanding these helps in
designing SCTAs that feel like helpful guidance rather than marketing noise.

## 1. Foot-in-the-Door Technique
This social psychology principle suggests that agreeing to a small request
(the SCTA) increases the likelihood of agreeing to a larger request (the Primary
CTA) later. By completing a "Micro-Commitment," the user starts to view
themselves as a "customer" or "user" of the brand, reducing the psychological
barrier to the next step.

## 2. Risk Reversal & Friction Matching
Conversion is a balance of **Value vs. Friction**.
- **Primary CTA:** High Value, High Friction (Requires time, money, or trust).
- **Secondary CTA:** Medium Value, Low Friction (Requires only attention).
By matching the friction of the ask to the current level of user trust, you
eliminate "The Wall" and create "The Slide."

## 3. The Zeigarnik Effect
This principle states that people remember uncompleted or interrupted tasks
better than completed ones. A well-designed SCTA (like a "Product Tour" or
"Savings Calculator") starts a "loop" in the user's mind. Once they see the
initial results, they feel a psychological urge to complete the process by
taking the next step (the Primary CTA).

## 4. Hick's Law and Choice Architecture
Hick's Law states that the time it takes to make a decision increases with the
number and complexity of choices. This is why **Subordination** is critical. If
you present two equal choices, you increase the cognitive load. By making the
Primary CTA dominant and the Secondary CTA a quiet alternative, you provide an
"escape hatch" for the hesitant user without confusing the ready buyer.

## 5. Information Scent (Scent of Information)
Users follow a "scent" toward their goal. If the Primary CTA feels like a
"dead-end" (e.g., they aren't ready to talk to sales), the SCTA acts as a
refinement of that scent. It keeps the user on the trail of information without
forcing them to jump to a conclusion too early.

## 6. The Goal Gradient Effect
The closer someone is to a goal, the more effort they will put in to reach it.
An SCTA should ideally show the user how close they are to the result. For
example, a "ROI Calculator" SCTA shows them the *result* they could achieve,
making the "Start Trial" Primary CTA feel like the final, necessary step to
realize that value.
