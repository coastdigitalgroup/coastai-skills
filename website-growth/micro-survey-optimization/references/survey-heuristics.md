# Psychology & UX Principles for Micro-Surveys

Optimizing micro-surveys requires understanding the psychological state of a
user who is interrupted during a task. Use these principles to guide your
designs.

## 1. The Principle of Reciprocity (Value Exchange)
Users are more likely to provide feedback if they feel they are helping a
product they value, or if they feel heard.
- **Action:** Frame the request as "Help us make this better for you" rather
  than "Help us improve our conversion rate."

## 2. Cognitive Load & Hick's Law
The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity
of choices.
- **Action:** Limit the initial step to 3–5 choices. Avoid asking for an
  open-ended essay response as the first interaction.

## 3. The Zeigarnik Effect
People remember uncompleted or interrupted tasks better than completed ones.
Once someone starts a survey, they have a psychological drive to finish it.
- **Action:** Use a progress bar for 2+ step surveys. If they click the first
  option, they are 90% likely to finish the second.

## 4. The Sunk Cost Fallacy (Commitment & Consistency)
Once a user has invested even 2 seconds in a survey, they are more likely to
continue to justify that initial investment.
- **Action:** Ask the easiest, most "non-threatening" question first.

## 5. Decision Fatigue
If a user is on a pricing page, they are already making high-stakes decisions.
An intrusive survey can push them into "avoidance" mode.
- **Action:** Use subtle, non-blocking UI (like a bottom-right "toast" or slide-in)
  rather than a full-screen "interstitial" modal.

## 6. Contextual Priming
Users' thoughts are dominated by the content they are currently viewing.
- **Action:** If they are on a "Returns & Shipping" page, the survey should
  only ask about "Shipping concerns," not "General satisfaction."

## 7. Peak-End Rule
People judge an experience based on how they felt at its peak and its end.
- **Action:** The "End" of the survey should be a positive, reinforcing
  experience (e.g., "Thanks! Your feedback helps us improve for everyone").
