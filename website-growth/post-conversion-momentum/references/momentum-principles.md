# Psychological Principles of Post-Conversion Momentum

Optimizing the "Success State" is not just about adding buttons; it is about
leveraging how the human brain processes completion and anticipation.

## 1. The Peak-End Rule
**The Principle:** People judge an experience largely based on how they felt at
its peak (the most intense point) and at its end, rather than the total sum or
average of every moment of the experience.
- **Application:** The conversion is often the "peak." If the "end" (the success
  page) is boring or confusing, it devalues the entire journey in the user's
  memory. High-momentum pages ensure the "end" feels as rewarding as the "peak."

## 2. The Zeigarnik Effect
**The Principle:** People remember uncompleted or interrupted tasks better than
completed ones.
- **Application:** While we want to confirm the *first* task is complete, we can
  immediately open a "new" task (the secondary CTA). This creates a psychological
  tension that the user wants to resolve by clicking the next button.

## 3. The Goal-Gradient Effect
**The Principle:** The closer people are to a goal, the harder they work to
achieve it.
- **Application:** Show the user how far they've come. "Step 1 (Signup) Complete.
  Step 2 (Personalize) is 50% done." This makes the secondary action feel like
  finishing a journey rather than starting a new one.

## 4. Choice Architecture: The Power of One
**The Principle:** Offering too many choices leads to "Decision Fatigue" and
inaction.
- **Application:** On a success page, the user has already spent cognitive
  energy. Protect their remaining energy by offering only one clear, high-value
  momentum trigger.

## 5. Reciprocity
**The Principle:** If you do something helpful for someone, they feel a natural
urge to do something helpful for you.
- **Application:** Because you just gave the user value (the download or the
  product), they are in a state of high reciprocity. This is the best moment to
  ask for a small "favor," like a referral or a social follow.
