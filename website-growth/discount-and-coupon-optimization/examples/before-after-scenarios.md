# Discount Optimization Examples

The following examples demonstrate the application of the Discount and Coupon
Optimization skill to real-world e-commerce scenarios.

## Scenario 1: Transitioning from Flat Discounts to Tiered Rewards

### The Problem
A boutique home goods store was offering a flat "10% Off Your First Order"
coupon to all new subscribers. While sign-ups were high, the Average Order Value
(AOV) was stagnant at $55, which barely covered customer acquisition and
shipping costs.

### The Optimization
The store replaced the flat 10% discount with a tiered "Spend & Save" progress
bar in the cart:
- **Tier 1:** Free Shipping on orders over $60.
- **Tier 2:** $10 Off orders over $85.
- **Tier 3:** $25 Off orders over $150.

### The Measurable Outcome
- **AOV Lift:** Increased from $55 to $72 (+31%).
- **Conversion Rate:** Remained stable, indicating the higher entry point did
  not deter high-intent users.
- **Net Profit per Order:** Increased by 18% as the higher AOV better
  distributed fixed shipping costs.

## Scenario 2: Subordinating the Coupon Field to Reduce Exit-Intent

### The Problem
An apparel brand noticed a 25% drop-off rate at the "Payment Information" step
of their checkout. Heatmap data showed users repeatedly clicking into the large,
prominent "Enter Promo Code" box, then leaving the site.

### The Optimization
The "Promo Code" input was removed from the primary visual hierarchy. It was
replaced with a subtle, collapsed text link that read: *"Have a promo code?"*
Additionally, for users arriving from an email campaign, the discount was
automatically applied via a URL parameter, with a "Discount Applied" success
message appearing in the order summary.

### The Measurable Outcome
- **Checkout Exit Rate:** Decreased from 25% to 16% (-36% reduction in
  abandonment).
- **Time to Complete Checkout:** Decreased by 12 seconds on average.
- **Revenue per Visitor (RPV):** Increased by 8% as fewer users "lost their
  way" hunting for codes.

## Scenario 3: Applying the "Rule of 100" to Clearance Items

### The Problem
An electronics retailer was struggling to move two items: a $45 phone case and a
$1,200 laptop. Both were advertised with a "15% Off" badge. The phone case was
selling well, but the laptop was not moving.

### The Optimization
Following the **Rule of 100**, the retailer reframed the laptop discount:
- **Phone Case ($45):** Kept as **"20% Off"** (Higher perceived value than "$9
  Off").
- **Laptop ($1,200):** Changed from "15% Off" to **"$180 Off"** (Higher
  perceived value than "15%").

### The Measurable Outcome
- **Laptop Sales Volume:** Increased by 42% in the first week after the
  reframing.
- **Click-Through Rate (CTR) from PLP:** Increased by 15% for high-ticket
  items.
- **Customer Sentiment:** Post-purchase surveys indicated users felt they got a
  "significant deal" on the laptop compared to previous weeks.
